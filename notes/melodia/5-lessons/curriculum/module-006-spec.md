## CEFR Level
**A1** — confirmed. Ser vs Estar is the single most defining grammar concept of A1 Spanish. Every CEFR A1 framework (Cervantes Plan Curricular, Common European Framework) places this contrast in the first 100 hours of instruction. Without it, the learner cannot say *I am tired*, *I am a teacher*, *she is at home*, or *the food is cold* — four core A1 functions.

## Concept
**Ser vs Estar — the two Spanish verbs for "to be."**

English has one verb (*to be*). Spanish has two. The split is not arbitrary — it maps onto a real cognitive distinction the learner needs to internalize: **identity vs. state**, or more memorably, **who you are vs. how you are.**

- **SER** → permanent / defining traits: name, nationality, profession, physical description, relationships, time/date, material.
- **ESTAR** → temporary states / locations / current conditions: feelings, mood, location, weather, ongoing actions.

The lesson teaches the contrast through the present-tense singular forms (the only forms a beginner needs to start producing real sentences this week).

## Builds on
- **Module 1 — Spanish Alphabet:** the rolled *rr* in *eres* (you are), the soft *s* in *soy*, *somos*, *estás* — pronunciation foundation matters here because *está* (he/she is) vs *esta* (this, fem.) is a Module-2 accent contrast that suddenly matters.
- **Module 2 — Accents & Stress:** *está* (verb, with accent) vs *esta* (demonstrative, no accent) — the accent is grammatical, not decorative. *Estás* (you are) vs *estas* (these, fem.) — same rule.
- **Module 5 — Subject Pronouns:** the learner already has *yo, tú, él, ella, nosotros, ellos*. Module 6 plugs the first real verb behind each pronoun. *Yo soy*, *tú eres*, *él/ella es*, *yo estoy*, *tú estás*, *él/ella está*. This is the exact moment pronouns stop being labels and start doing work.

## Functional speaking goal
**By the end of this lesson, the learner can:**
1. Introduce themselves with a permanent trait: **"Soy [name]."** / **"Soy de [country]."** / **"Soy estudiante."**
2. Describe how they feel right now: **"Estoy cansado/cansada."** / **"Estoy feliz."** / **"Estoy bien."**
3. State where they (or someone else) currently is: **"Estoy en casa."** / **"Ella está aquí."**
4. Make the **identity vs state** judgment in real time — pick *ser* for who someone is, *estar* for how they are or where they are.
5. Recognize the contrast in a song lyric: when an artist sings *estoy*, they're telling you about a *feeling or place right now*; when they sing *soy*, they're claiming an *identity*.

This is the first module where the learner produces **two genuinely different sentences** about themselves — one about identity, one about state. That's a real conversation starter.

## speakingGoal
**"After this lesson, the learner can say two sentences about themselves out loud:**
1. **Soy [name / nationality / role].** — who I am.
2. **Estoy [feeling / location].** — how I feel or where I am right now.

**And they can hear the difference between *soy* and *estoy* in a song without thinking about it."**

## listeningSkill
Train the ear to catch the **soy / estoy** contrast at speed. In real Spanish lyrics:
- *soy* is short and clipped — one syllable, pure vowels: *soy*.
- *estoy* is two syllables with the stress on the second: *es-TOY*.
- *es* is the easiest to miss — a single short syllable, often unstressed, gluing the sentence together.
- *está* (with the stressed final *á*) jumps out — the accent is audible.

The learner's listening task during the song: **count how many times the artist uses estar forms (estoy, estás, está) versus ser forms (soy, eres, es).** This trains them to hear the verb-of-the-moment vs. the verb-of-identity even when the lyrics fly past.

## key contrast pairs
The single biggest pedagogical lever in this module — **identity vs state** demonstrated through minimal pairs that change meaning when the verb changes:

| Identity (SER) | State (ESTAR) | What flips |
|---|---|---|
| **Soy alto.** — I am tall. | **Estoy alto.** — I'm standing tall right now (e.g., wearing heels, on a stage). | Permanent trait → temporary stance |
| **Soy feliz.** — I am a happy person (by nature). | **Estoy feliz.** — I'm happy right now (in this moment). | Disposition → current mood |
| **Es aburrido.** — He is boring (as a person). | **Está aburrido.** — He is bored (right now). | Trait → state |
| **Soy de México.** — I'm from Mexico (origin). | **Estoy en México.** — I'm in Mexico (right now). | Origin → location |
| **Eres bonita.** — You are beautiful (always). | **Estás bonita.** — You look beautiful (tonight / today). | Identity-level compliment → moment-specific compliment |

**Teach 2-3 of these pairs in the lesson body. The "soy feliz / estoy feliz" pair is the most pedagogically clean** — same adjective, same translation in English, the only thing that changes is *which Spanish "to be" you reach for* and *what it means*. Use this as the anchor contrast.

**The four-bucket mnemonic (optional teaching aid, content-builder discretion):**
- **SER** = **DOCTOR** (Description, Occupation, Characteristic, Time, Origin, Relationship)
- **ESTAR** = **PLACE** (Position, Location, Action, Condition, Emotion)

These mnemonics are standard in Spanish pedagogy and load-bearing for the learner — but they're English-language scaffolding. Don't quiz the mnemonics; quiz the *judgment*.

**Review timing rationale:**
- **1 day** — the soy/estoy contrast is fragile in working memory; an overnight review cements the *sound* difference before the meanings get tangled.
- **3 days** — accent contrasts (está/esta, estás/estas) peak in confusion around day 3 — one targeted recall here saves weeks of muddled writing.
- **7 days** — the ser/estar judgment ("which verb do I pick?") needs a week of spacing to become automatic; one targeted recall here is more effective than three in a row.
- **14 days** — accents fade fastest; one more reinforcement at two weeks locks them.
- **30 days** — the pronoun + verb scaffold (M5 + M6 combined) is the bedrock for every future verb module; deep-spaced review here pays compounding dividends.

## culturalNoteDirection

**One-paragraph direction for the content-builder (the final note will be original writing, 4-5 sentences, in the M5 narration style):**

The note should answer the question the learner is already silently asking: **why does Spanish need two "to be" verbs at all?** Frame it as a *feature*, not a quirk. The split between *ser* and *estar* lets a Spanish speaker say things English cannot say in one word — the difference between *who you are* and *where you are in this moment* is baked into the grammar. Anchor it culturally to the song chosen by the song-validator. If the song is **"Estoy Aquí" by Shakira** (the recommended primary, see below), the note should make the title itself the lesson — *estoy aquí* uses *estar* twice over: *estar* for state ("I am [waiting / longing]") and *estar* for location ("I am here"). Shakira's choice of *estoy* over *soy* is the whole emotional weight of the song — she's not telling us *who she is*, she's telling us *where she is and how she feels right now*. That's exactly the ser/estar contrast in action. The note should close with a listening cue: "Every time you hear *estoy* in a Latin song, the artist is letting you in on a *moment*. Every time you hear *soy*, they're claiming an *identity*. Those two verbs are how Spanish lets a singer be both at once."

## Phrase chunk

**Primary: "Estoy bien, ¿y tú?"** — *I'm well, and you?*

This is the single most useful exchange a beginner can leave with. It:
- Uses **estoy** (form of *estar*, state) in real spoken context.
- Recycles **tú** from M5 — a pronoun the learner already has.
- Recycles **y** from M4 (the connector "and").
- Recycles **¿** punctuation and question-intonation from M3.
- Is the textbook answer to "¿Cómo estás?" — the most-asked question in Spanish-speaking interactions.

**Secondary phrase chunk (for variety in quizzes):**
**"Soy [name]. Estoy cansado/cansada."** — *I'm [name]. I'm tired.* Two sentences, one identity (ser), one state (estar) — back-to-back proof that the learner can deploy both verbs.

## Speaking prompt

**"Look at yourself in the mirror. Say two sentences out loud. First, a SER sentence: *Soy [your name]*. Then, an ESTAR sentence: *Estoy [how you feel right now]*. If you're tired, say *estoy cansado* (or *estoy cansada* — feminine ending). If you're happy, *estoy feliz*. If you're good, *estoy bien*. Same person, two verbs, two different things being said. That's the whole lesson."**

Why this works:
- **Identity + state in the same breath** — physically rehearses the contrast on the same human (the learner), so the distinction is felt, not just memorized.
- **Personalizable** — the name and feeling are theirs, so the sentence sticks.
- **Mirror anchor** — the learner sees themselves while making the identity vs state distinction. Pedagogically this binds the abstract concept to a concrete referent.

## quizConceptSeeds (5 — content-builder writes the actual questions)

1. **Pure recognition (easy tier):** Match *soy* to "I am (identity)" and *estoy* to "I am (state/location)." Foundational — the learner has to lock the contrast before anything else works.

2. **Verb-pick judgment (standard tier):** Given a context sentence in English ("She is a doctor" / "She is tired" / "He is in the kitchen" / "We are from Argentina"), the learner picks **ser** or **estar**. This is the core skill the module is teaching — five or six of these in a row in the standard quiz.

3. **Conjugation match (standard tier):** Given a pronoun (yo / tú / él / ella) and a verb (ser or estar), the learner picks the correct form (soy/eres/es OR estoy/estás/está). This recycles M5 pronouns into M6 verbs in the cleanest possible way.

4. **Minimal-pair meaning flip (hard tier):** Show *Soy feliz* and *Estoy feliz* side by side and ask the learner what changes. Correct answer: both translate as "I am happy," but *soy feliz* claims happiness as part of your identity (you're a happy person by nature), while *estoy feliz* describes how you feel in this moment. This is the highest-value conceptual question in the module.

5. **Listening / phrase-chunk recall (hard tier):** Translate "I'm well, and you?" → *Estoy bien, ¿y tú?* — tests integration of M6 (estoy), M5 (tú), M4 (y), and M3 (¿?) in one fluid chunk. If the learner can produce this, they can survive their first 30 seconds of small talk in Spanish.

## Quiz concept coverage breakdown

The content-builder should produce **3 easy / 4 standard / 3 hard** questions covering:
- **Easy:** soy/estoy recognition; ser/estar infinitive recognition; eres vs estás recognition.
- **Standard:** ser-or-estar context picks (×3); pronoun-to-conjugation match.
- **Hard:** minimal-pair meaning flip (*soy feliz* vs *estoy feliz*); full-sentence translation including phrase chunk; identification of which verb a song lyric is using and why.

**Reading comprehension level:** **88% English / 12% Spanish.** Slightly more Spanish than M5 because the learner now has a base of pronouns AND can recognize *soy / estoy / eres / es / está*. The 12% Spanish in the reading passage should be **only**: *soy*, *estoy*, *eres*, *es*, *está*, *aquí*, plus the song title *Estoy Aquí*. **No new Spanish vocab in the reading unless tappable with inline translation.**
