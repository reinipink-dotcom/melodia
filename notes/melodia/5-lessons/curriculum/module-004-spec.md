# Module 4 — Curriculum Spec

## 1. CEFR Level
**A1** (confirmed). This module sits correctly at A1 — these are high-frequency function words that A1 learners need before they can build any real sentence beyond labels and greetings. No grammar conjugation is required; the cognitive load is purely lexical recognition + slot-filling.

## 2. Core Grammar Concept + Vocabulary Theme

**Grammar concept:** Function words — the small, unchanging "glue" between content words. Three sub-families introduced together:
- **Prepositions** (relationships between things): `de`, `con`, `sin`
- **Conjunctions** (joining ideas): `pero`
- **Adverbs of inclusion** (adding/relating): `también`

**Vocabulary theme:** Sentence connectors that appear in nearly every Spanish lyric and conversation. The five core words are deliberately high-frequency and low-ambiguity — each has one dominant meaning at A1.

**Why this combination at Module 4:** The learner already knows letters (M1), accent rules (M2), and question words (M3). They can read Spanish out loud and ask basic questions. Module 4 gives them the connective tissue to start *understanding* short lyric lines like "café con leche," "sin ti," "yo también" — wins they can feel inside a real song within minutes.

## 3. Functional Speaking Goal

**After this lesson, learners can:**
> Connect two ideas in a simple Spanish sentence using *con*, *sin*, *pero*, and *también* — and recognize *de* as the "of/from" link inside names, places, and short phrases.

Concretely, the learner walks away able to say things like:
- "Café con leche, sin azúcar." (Coffee with milk, no sugar.)
- "Yo también." (Me too.)
- "Soy de Colombia, pero hablo inglés." (I'm from Colombia, but I speak English.)

This is the first module where the learner produces a **two-clause sentence**. That is the milestone.

## 4. Listening Skill Focus

**Target skill:** Catch short, single-syllable connectors as the song moves quickly past them. These words almost never carry the emphasis in a lyric — they sit between stressed syllables. The ear has to learn that *con*, *sin*, *de*, *y* often blend into the surrounding word.

**Specific listening cues:**
- Hear *con* and *sin* as functional opposites (with / without)
- Notice *de* inside place names and possession ("la voz de Colombia")
- Catch *también* as a full stand-alone reaction word
- Distinguish *pero* (but) from *perro* (dog) — the trilled R is the only difference

## 5. Core Vocabulary (5 words)

| Spanish | English | Pronunciation hook |
|---|---|---|
| **de** | of / from | A single soft "deh" — almost swallowed in fast speech |
| **con** | with | Rhymes with English "cone" minus the final E |
| **sin** | without | Like the English word "seen" but shorter |
| **pero** | but | Tap the R once — soft, not rolled |
| **también** | also / too | Stress on the final "bién" — accent mark required |

## 6. Bonus Vocabulary (3 stretch words)

| Spanish | English | Why it earns the slot |
|---|---|---|
| **y** | and | The most-used word in any language; ridiculous to skip |
| **o** | or | Pairs naturally with *y* — gives learners a choice connector |
| **nunca** | never | High-frequency adverb that appears in dozens of song titles |

*Note: tampoco and siempre from the original grammarPoints are deferred to Module 7 (where negation gets its own treatment) to avoid overloading this lesson.*

## 7. Phrase Chunk

**"Café con leche, sin azúcar."**
*(Coffee with milk, no sugar.)*

Six words. Two prepositions. One drink that exists in every Spanish-speaking country. This is the chunk the learner repeats out loud, hears in the Juanes track's gentler verses, and can use the next morning at any café.

Secondary chunk for the speaking pattern: **"Yo también."** *(Me too.)* — the most useful two-word response in Spanish.

## 8. Speaking Prompt

**Mini-challenge for the learner:**
> Order your morning drink in Spanish. Pick your real preferences and say it out loud: "Café / té / agua, con / sin _____, pero _____."

Example fillable frames the content builder should generate:
- "Quiero café ___ leche." (con / sin)
- "Hablo inglés, ___ no español." (pero)
- "Yo ___." (también — as a response to "I love music")

## 9. Cultural Note (130 words — original writing)

**Title: Juanes and the Sound of Connection**

Juan Esteban Aristizábal Vásquez — known to the world as Juanes — grew up in Medellín during one of Colombia's hardest decades. His songs almost always link two worlds: rock and Colombian folk, English and Spanish audiences, pain and hope. Listen to "A Dios Le Pido" and you'll hear the small words doing heavy lifting — *con*, *sin*, *de*, *para* — stitching together a prayer that asks for life *con* his loved ones and *sin* fear. That is exactly what these glue words do inside the language: they hold opposite ideas in the same breath. Juanes also founded Mi Sangre, a foundation supporting children affected by landmines. The connection between his music and his peace work is not a coincidence — both are built on the same idea of joining what war tries to separate.

## 11. Notes for the Content Builder

**What makes Module 4 unique:**

1. **First true sentence-building module.** Modules 1-3 taught the alphabet, accents, and questions in isolation. Module 4 is the first time the learner produces a complete A-then-B Spanish sentence. The lesson should *celebrate this* in the lesson-complete screen ("You just connected two ideas in Spanish for the first time").

2. **Pero vs perro is a teaching moment, not a trap.** Lean into the contrast — make a quiz question that puts them side by side. The learner remembers it forever after one laugh.

3. **The Juanes cultural note must land before the quiz, not after.** This is the module where cultural context starts doing real motivational work. Reading about Juanes's peace foundation should reframe the small words *con* and *sin* as carrying weight — "with my loved ones, without fear" — not as grammar trivia.

4. **Avoid introducing *por* and *para*.** The original grammarPoints mentioned them as a teaser for Module 41. Keep them out of the quiz entirely — they will confuse beginners who are still consolidating five new words. A single sentence in the conceptDescription pointing forward to Module 41 is fine.

5. **Reading ratio 90/10 is correct.** The 10% Spanish should be exactly the 5 core words sprinkled into an otherwise English passage about Juanes. Do not stretch the Spanish percentage just because the topic is rich — the learner is still A1.

6. **Quiz types confirmed:** Fill-in-the-preposition, match connectors to meaning, sentence building. Recommend adding one **listening question** — play a 5-second clip and ask "Did you hear *con* or *sin*?"

7. **Phrase chunk goes everywhere.** "Café con leche, sin azúcar" should appear in pre-listening context, the quiz, the reading passage callback, and the lesson-complete celebration. Repetition across screens is the spaced repetition.
