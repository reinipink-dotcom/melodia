# Module 1 — Curriculum Spec

## 1. CEFR Level
**A1** (confirmed — entry point of the curriculum). Module 1 sits at the absolute floor of A1. The cognitive load is purely phonetic recognition — no conjugation, no syntax, no gendered nouns. The learner is being handed the sound system of the language before any word-meaning is required. This is the correct way into a phonetic language like Spanish: pronunciation first, vocabulary as a side-effect of the sound work.

A1 fit is reinforced by the fact that the five vocab entries are **letters and digraphs**, not words. The learner is not yet asked to remember meaning — only to map a written symbol to a stable sound. That is the lightest possible A1 ask.

## 2. Core Grammar Concept + Vocabulary Theme

**Grammar concept (technically "phonetics," but framed as grammar for the curriculum):** The Spanish alphabet is 27 letters — the 26 English letters plus **ñ**. Spanish is near-perfectly phonetic: every letter maps to one consistent sound. Once the learner internalizes the five vowel sounds and a handful of distinctive consonants, they can read any Spanish word aloud correctly, even without knowing what it means.

**Vocabulary theme:** The five highest-leverage sound entries — the ones that produce 90% of pronunciation confusion for English speakers in the first month. These five are **confirmed correct**:

1. **a, e, i, o, u** — the 5 pure vowels (taught as a unit because they always behave the same way)
2. **ñ** — uniquely Spanish, instantly recognizable, marks identity of the alphabet
3. **ll** — the digraph that breaks the English "double L = same L" expectation
4. **h** — the silent letter that learners hyper-pronounce by default
5. **rr** — the trilled R, the most-asked-about sound in Spanish

**Why these five:** Each entry corrects a specific English-speaker bias. Vowels fix the diphthong habit ("eh-ee" → just "e"). Ñ is the identity letter. LL, H, and RR each unlock dozens of high-frequency words (*llamar, hola, perro*). Adding more (e.g., j, c, g, z) would dilute the lesson and push it past A1 cognitive load. The five chosen are the **right** five.

## 3. Functional Speaking Goal

**After this lesson, learners can:**
> Read any written Spanish word aloud with correct, recognizable pronunciation — even words they have never seen before — and recognize the five problem-letter sounds (ñ, ll, h, rr, pure vowels) when they hear them.

Concretely, the learner walks away able to:
- Say their own name and any English name "in Spanish" (María, José, Sofía — vowel-pure)
- Pronounce *hola* without an H sound
- Tell that *año* (year) and *ano* (anus) are different words because of ñ — a small joke that makes the letter unforgettable
- Trill, or at least tap, the double R in *perro*

The milestone framing for the lesson-complete screen: **"You can now read Spanish out loud. That's the door. Everything from here is words behind it."**

## 4. Listening Skill Focus

**Target skill:** Train the ear to expect **pure, unchanging vowels**. English ears default to gliding diphthongs (the English "a" in "cake" is actually "ay-ee"). Spanish vowels do not glide. The whole module hinges on this single ear-shift.

**Specific listening cues in Bésame Mucho:**
- The held **é** in *bésame* — a single steady vowel, not a slide
- The **mu** in *mucho* — pure "moo," not "myoo"
- The silent **h** elsewhere in Spanish (not in this song's title, but called out in reading)
- Vowel clarity even when the bolero rhythm slows the syllables to almost a whisper

The learner is not yet expected to catch words for meaning. They are listening for **sound shapes**.

## 5. Core Vocabulary (5 entries — confirmed)

| Entry | Sound | Pronunciation hook |
|---|---|---|
| **a, e, i, o, u** | ah / eh / ee / oh / oo | Always pure, always the same — no gliding |
| **ñ** | "ny" as in *canyon* | The tilde is not decoration — it's a different letter |
| **ll** | "y" as in *yes* | Looks like English LL, sounds nothing like it |
| **h** | silent | Always silent, no exceptions at A1 |
| **rr** | rolled / trilled R | Two R's = roll; one R between vowels = a soft tap |

**Quiz vocabulary constraint:** Module 1 is the first module. There is no prior vocabulary to draw on. Every Spanish word that appears in a quiz question must come from this module's vocabulary set OR from the song title itself (*Bésame Mucho*) OR from the example words listed in the vocabulary entries (*niño, mañana, año, llamar, llevar, llorar, hola, hablar, hacer, perro, carro, tierra*). No other Spanish words may be introduced in quiz questions. This is a hard constraint for the content builder.

## 6. Bonus Vocabulary (3 stretch words — meaningful payoff)

These are full **words** (not just letters) that the learner can recognize by the end of the lesson because they only use sounds from the core five:

| Spanish | English | Why it earns the slot |
|---|---|---|
| **hola** | hello | The universal first Spanish word — and it proves H is silent |
| **mucho** | a lot / very much | Already in the song title; one of the first Spanish words English speakers half-know |
| **sí** | yes | One syllable, one pure vowel, an accent mark teaser for Module 2 |

These three are deliberately small. The point of Module 1 is **sounds**, not vocabulary. Three real words is enough payoff without diluting the lesson.

## 7. Phrase Chunk

**"Bésame mucho."**
*(Kiss me a lot.)*

The lesson's phrase chunk **is the song title** — and that is the entire point. Two words. Five syllables. Contains a pure É, a silent inheritance (the soft B/V we mention), and the pure U in *mucho*. The learner ends the lesson able to pronounce a real, romantic, century-old Spanish phrase correctly. That is a disproportionate emotional win for a first lesson.

Secondary chunk for everyday use: **"Hola."** — one word, but the silent H makes it a teaching artifact.

## 8. Speaking Prompt

**Mini-challenge for the learner:**
> Say the five vowels out loud, slowly: **a — e — i — o — u**. Then say your own name as if you were Spanish. Then say "Bésame mucho." That's it. Three things, out loud, before the quiz.

The speaking prompt at Module 1 is deliberately gentle. We are not asking the learner to produce a sentence — we are asking them to **make sound with their mouth** and notice it feels different from English. The bar is low on purpose, because the cost of a beginner clamming up on day one is the whole curriculum.

Fillable frames for the content builder:
- "Read out loud: *hola, hablar, hacer.*" (H is silent in all three)
- "Read out loud: *llamar, llevar, llorar.*" (LL is "y")
- "Try the roll: *perro, carro, tierra.*" (a tap is fine — perfection not required)

## 9. Cultural Note (Consuelo Velázquez — direction + scope)

**Title: The Girl Who Wrote Bésame Mucho**

**Facts confirmed (verifiable, public-domain biography):**
- Consuelo Velázquez Torres, Mexican composer and pianist
- Born 1916 in Ciudad Guzmán, Jalisco; died 2005 in Mexico City
- Composed "Bésame Mucho" around 1940 in Mexico City, in her late teens (commonly cited as 16, with some sources saying she was 19 by the time it was published — both ages have appeared in press; **flagging this minor ambiguity for the content builder to phrase carefully**)
- Famously said she had never been kissed when she wrote it
- The song became one of the most-recorded songs in history, translated into 20+ languages
- Recorded by Frank Sinatra, The Beatles, Plácido Domingo, Cesária Évora and many more
- Velázquez later served in Mexican public office and was a member of SACM (the Mexican composers' society)

**Scope for the note (130-150 words, original writing):**
- Lead with the image: a teenage girl at a piano in 1940 Mexico City
- Connect to the lesson: the song is built almost entirely from pure-vowel Spanish sounds — *bésame, mucho, siempre, miedo* — making it a perfect first-lesson listening text
- Land on identity: this is a Mexican song, in Spanish, written by a woman, that the whole world ended up singing. That is what Spanish music does — it travels.
- **Tone:** Warm, music-anchored, never patronizing. The learner is meeting a real composer, not a flashcard.

**What the cultural note should NOT do:**
- Quote any lyrics beyond the title (copyright + style guide)
- Invent biographical details
- Mention the kiss anecdote without the public-source framing
- Drift into music history beyond what connects back to Spanish sounds

**Ambiguity flag for content builder:** When stating Velázquez's age at composition, prefer "a teenager" or "in her late teens" over a hard "at 16" unless the content builder verifies against a specific cited source. The 16 figure is widely repeated but not universally agreed.

## 11. Quiz Vocabulary Constraint (HARD RULE)

Module 1 is the entry point. There is no prior Spanish vocabulary to draw on. The content builder must follow this rule when generating `easyQuizQuestions` and `hardQuizQuestions`:

**Allowed Spanish words in quizzes:**
- The five vowel letters and the four digraph/letter entries (a, e, i, o, u, ñ, ll, h, rr)
- The song title: *Bésame Mucho*, and its component words *bésame, mucho*
- The example words already listed in `vocabulary[].english` descriptions: *niño, mañana, año, llamar, llevar, llorar, hola, hablar, hacer, hay, perro, carro, tierra, lluvia*
- The three bonus words: *hola, mucho, sí*

**Forbidden:** Any other Spanish word. No *gracias*, no *amor*, no *cómo estás*. The learner has not been given those yet and quizzing on them violates the spaced-repetition logic of the curriculum.

**English in quizzes is unrestricted** — the question stems, explanations, and meaning glosses can use any English.

## 13. Notes for the Content Builder

**What makes Module 1 unique:**

1. **There is no "before."** Every other module gets to recycle prior vocabulary. Module 1 has nothing behind it. Lean into this — the lesson should feel like an opening, not a checklist. The pre-listening screen should explicitly say something like "This is your first Spanish lesson. The only goal today is to start hearing the language correctly."

2. **The phrase chunk IS the song title.** Don't invent a different chunk. *Bésame mucho* is the chunk. It does double duty as the song the learner is about to listen to, the cultural anchor, and the pronunciation showcase.

3. **Do not quiz on meaning yet.** Spanish-to-English translation questions are appropriate for the bonus words (*hola, mucho, sí*) only. The other quiz questions should be about sound, stress, syllables, and letter identification — not meaning. The existing draft `quizQuestions` in modules.ts is well-aimed and can serve as the model for tone.

4. **The B/V softness is fair game.** Even though B and V are not in the official 5 vocab entries, the first existing quiz question covers them because the song title starts with B. That is acceptable — the song forces it. Limit this to one question.

5. **The trilled R is a stretch, not a requirement.** Beginners who can't roll their R's must not feel like they failed Module 1. The quiz should test recognition ("which word has the rolled R?") not production. Reassure the learner in the explanation: "A single tap is fine — Spanish speakers will understand you."

6. **The cultural note runs BEFORE the quiz, AFTER the song.** This order matters: the learner reads about Consuelo *after* hearing her song, so the bio lands with the music still in their head.

7. **The lesson-complete screen should celebrate one thing only:** "You can now read any Spanish word out loud." Do not list every sub-skill. One clean win.

8. **Reading passage already drafted in modules.ts is high quality.** It can be kept largely as-is. The content builder's job for reading is to verify it stays under 220 words, uses exactly 5 highlighted Spanish words, and that the Consuelo facts match the ambiguity flag in section 9 above.
